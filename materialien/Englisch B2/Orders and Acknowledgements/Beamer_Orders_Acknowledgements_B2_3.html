Definitions

What are Orders & Acknowledgements?

📄
Order (Bestellung)

A formal request to purchase specific goods or services — usually placed after accepting a supplier's offer. Once acknowledged by the seller, a binding contract exists between both parties.

✅
Acknowledgement of Order (Auftragsbestätigung)

The seller's written confirmation that the order has been received and accepted. It must confirm all key details — article, quantity, price, delivery terms, and payment.

⚠️
Key Legal Point

Any discrepancy between the order and the acknowledgement must be flagged immediately. Silence is treated as acceptance of the seller's revised terms.

Four Types of Orders

tap
Trial Order
Probeauftrag
Testing a new supplier before committing to larger volumes.
"We would like to place a trial order for 60 chairs to assess quality before committing to a regular supply agreement."
tap
Repeat Order
Nachbestellung
Reordering identical goods from a known supplier on the same terms.
"As the goods met our requirements, we would like to place a repeat order on the same terms as before."
tap
Standing Order
Dauerauftrag
Regular automatic delivery of identical goods at fixed intervals.
"Please arrange for monthly delivery of 20 units of ref. EP3-BLK on the first working day of each month."
tap
Call-off Order
Abrufauftrag
Total quantity agreed upfront; goods released in smaller batches.
"We confirm an annual call-off of 500 units. Please dispatch the first batch of 100 units by 15 December."

Structure of an Acknowledgement

1
Acknowledge receipt of order
›
'Thank you for your order no. OD-2024-047 of [date].'
2
Confirm all article details + discount
›
'We confirm: 60 × EP3-BLK @ £189.00, less 10% = £10,206.00.'
3
Delivery terms & expected dispatch
›

Use tentative language unless 100% certain.

'We expect to dispatch DDP Dusseldorf within 10 working days.'
4
Payment terms + enclosures
›
'Payment is due within 30 days of receipt of invoice. We enclose our pro-forma invoice.'
!
If unable to fulfil the order
›
  • Inform the buyer immediately
  • Explain the reason honestly
  • Offer a substitute, partial delivery, or revised date
'We regret to inform you that [item] is temporarily out of stock. We would like to suggest [alternative] as a substitute.'
B2 Style

Tentative vs. Definite Language

Use tentative language in acknowledgements when you are not 100% certain. Overpromising damages trust.

"The goods will be dispatched on 10 December."

→ Only if you are completely certain.

"We expect to dispatch within 10 working days."

"We anticipate dispatch by 15 December."

"If you can't deliver, tell us."

"Should you be unable to meet the delivery date, please inform us immediately."

💡
Grammar Pattern

Should + subject + infinitive = formal alternative to if. Used for polite, hypothetical conditions. Common in B2 business writing.
